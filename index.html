About Me

Welcome to my webpage! My name is Rosario Cecilio-Flores-Elie and I am a second-year PhD student in Geological Sciences at Cornell University.

Research Interests

I am interested in studying the geophysical mechanisms behind geyser activity on Earth and icy moons like Enceladus. Under the mentorship of Dr. Carolina Muñoz-Saez, I aim to use geophysical surveys to study geysers in El Tatio, Chile. I also plan to develop models to better understand the processes driving geyser activity on moons like Enceladus, which suggest the presence of a subsurface ocean. By examining the interaction between water and geological forces in these environments, I hope to deepen our understanding of geological activity and the potential for habitability on ocean worlds.

General Background

I am a first-generation Mexican-American from the Bronx. I love exploring new countries, indulging in diverse cuisines, staying active with swimming and weight-lifting, and cherishing moments with my husband and our beloved dog, Lenin.

Before pursuing Planetary Geophysics, I spent nine years as a second-grade bilingual teacher in the Bronx.

As a first-generation Latina, I deeply understand the challenges and barriers that can arise in academia. I am passionate about advancing diversity and inclusion in Astrophysics and Planetary Sciences, working to ensure that underrepresented and first-generation students have equitable access to opportunities that empower them to succeed and thrive.

TESS Light Curves

I have analyzed 100 light curves with data from NASA's TESS mission, a space telescope designed to discover exoplanets by observing star brightness changes during planetary transits. Beyond its primary mission, TESS provides a vast dataset of stellar light curves that reveal insights into stellar behavior.

My work focuses on young co-moving stars, utilizing TESS's light curves to determine rotational periods and identify indicators of activity like solar flares, rapid rotation, and potential binarity. By studying these features, I contribute to understanding the dynamics of these young stars, enriching our knowledge of their evolution and interactions.

Planet-Moon Interactions

Part of my master's thesis focused on mapping the mass ratios, binding energies, and orbital separations of planets and their moons. The study aimed to draw parallels between the geological activity observed on moons like Io, Europa, and Enceladus, driven by interactions with their parent planets, and similar dynamics observed in star-planet systems within and outside the habitable zone. Building on the work of Faherty et al., (2021) and Rothermich et al., (2024), my research extended the analysis to include systems that range from stellar to planetary.

Some highlighted results revealed a potential correlation between mass ratios and geological activity. Moons like Io, Europa, Ganymede, Titan, and Enceladus clustered around specific mass ratios (10⁻⁸ to 10⁻⁴) and separations (10⁻⁴AU to 10⁻²AU), while some exoplanets, such as Proxima b, TRAPPIST-1f, and Kepler-62f, exhibited similar mass ratios. These findings contribute to our understanding of planetary dynamics and the potential of ocean worlds both within our Solar System and beyond.

Near-Surface Geophysics

During the summer of 2021, I participated in the REU "Water Quality in the Yucatán Peninsula" program, led by Northern Illinois University.

The project focused on the Yucatán Peninsula, known for its vast karstic aquifer system supplying regional freshwater. I worked under Dr. Philip J. Carpenter, using specialized equipment like The SuperSting™ to conduct geophysical surveys near cenotes, sinkholes formed by collapsed limestone revealing groundwater.

These surveys helped us understand water flow, quality, and potential anthropogenic impacts due to tourism and migration. Notably, I discovered new locations, including Agua Azul, where I found water-filled conduits, fracture zones, and even an air cavity.

My findings were presented at the American Geophysical Union (AGU) and NDiSTEM - Society for Advancement of Chicanos/Hispanics & Native Americans in Science (SACNAS) conferences in 2021 and 2022, shedding light on the region's water dynamics and pollution concerns.

Teaching & Outreach

I hold a NYS teaching certification in Childhood Education (1st-6th) with a Bilingual Extension.

During my nine year tenure, I spearheaded the development of culturally-responsive literacy units for second grade. These units integrated urban experiences and cross-disciplinary content while utilizing scaffolding techniques to cater to diverse learners, including those with learning disabilities and multilingual learners.

Furthermore, I provided focused mentorship to 1-2 student-teachers annually. Through this mentorship, I shared specialized insights and pedagogical expertise, fostering their professional growth and advancement.

While I am no longer actively involved in classroom instruction, my commitment to cultivating a supportive environment for future students, mentees, and colleagues remains unwavering.
