About Me

Welcome to my webpage! My name is Rosario Cecilio-Flores-Elie and I am a second-year PhD student in Geological Sciences at Cornell University.

Research Interests

My research focuses on understanding the structural control of hydrothermal systems. Through detailed structural mapping and geophysical analysis, I investigate how faults and fractures influence fluid circulation around geysers. Currently, I am applying this approach to the El Tatio Geyser Field, which is the third-largest and highest-elevation geyser field in the world. The goal of my work is to gain insights into terrestrial geysers that can inform our understanding of hydrothermal activity on other planetary bodies, such as Enceladus and Triton.

General Background

I am a first-generation Mexican-American from the Bronx. I love exploring new countries, indulging in diverse cuisines, staying active with swimming and weight-lifting, and cherishing moments with my husband and our beloved dog, Lenin.

Before pursuing Planetary Geophysics, I spent nine years as a second-grade bilingual teacher in the Bronx.

As a first-generation Latina, I am aware of the challenges faced in academia. My passion lies in promoting diversity, equity, and inclusion within the fields of Astrophysics and Planetary Sciences. I am dedicated to ensuring that underrepresented and first-generation students have fair access to opportunities that enable them to succeed.

Research

My research focuses on how faults, fractures, and surface structural architecture control fluid pathways and hydrothermal surface expressions. Using detailed geological mapping, drone-derived imagery, and spatial analysis, I investigate how structural complexity governs where fluids reach the surface.

This work builds on a multidisciplinary background that includes near-surface geophysics, analysis of stellar photometric data from NASA’s TESS mission, and studies of planet–moon system dynamics. By applying terrestrial analogs such as the El Tatio geyser field to planetary contexts, my research informs interpretations of fracture-controlled plume and hydrothermal activity on icy ocean worlds including Enceladus and Titan.

TESS Light Curves

I analyzed photometric light curves from NASA’s Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS) to study stellar rotation and activity in young co-moving stars. This work leveraged TESS’s high-cadence observations to extract rotational periods and identify signatures of stellar activity, including flares, rapid rotation, and potential binarity.

By examining variability patterns across a sample of co-moving systems, this project contributed to characterizing the rotational and magnetic properties of young low-mass stars and their companions, providing insight into stellar evolution and system dynamics.

The results of this work were published in:

Rothermich, A., Faherty, J., et al. (including Cecilio-Flores-Elie, R.) (2024), 89 New Ultracool Dwarf Co-Moving Companions Identified with the Backyard Worlds: Planet 9 Citizen Science Project , The Astronomical Journal.

Planet-Moon Interactions

Part of my master's thesis focused on mapping the mass ratios, binding energies, and orbital separations of planets and their moons. The study aimed to draw parallels between the geological activity observed on moons like Io, Europa, and Enceladus, driven by interactions with their parent planets, and similar dynamics observed in star-planet systems within and outside the habitable zone. Building on the work of Faherty et al., (2021) and Rothermich et al., (2024), my research extended the analysis to include systems that range from stellar to planetary.

Some highlighted results revealed a potential correlation between mass ratios and geological activity. Moons like Io, Europa, Ganymede, Titan, and Enceladus clustered around specific mass ratios (10⁻⁸ to 10⁻⁴) and separations (10⁻⁴AU to 10⁻²AU), while some exoplanets, such as Proxima b, TRAPPIST-1f, and Kepler-62f, exhibited similar mass ratios. These findings contribute to our understanding of planetary dynamics and the potential of ocean worlds both within our Solar System and beyond.

Geysers in El Tatio, Chile

Under the advisement of Dr. Carolina Muñoz-Saez, my research focuses on understanding how faults and fracture networks control the spatial distribution and surface expression of hydrothermal activity at the El Tatio geyser field in northern Chile.

Using high-resolution drone-derived imagery, digital elevation models (DEMs), and detailed geological mapping, I characterize surface structures including faults, fractures, and zones of structural complexity and analyze their relationships to geysers, hot springs, fumaroles, and other hydrothermal manifestations. This work aims to identify the structural conditions that promote sustained fluid pathways and surface discharge.

El Tatio serves as a terrestrial analog for fracture-controlled plume and hydrothermal activity on icy ocean worlds such as Enceladus. By constraining how surface and near-surface structures influence fluid ascent on Earth, this research provides a framework for interpreting fracture networks, plume sources, and potential habitable environments on other planetary bodies.

Near-Surface Geophysics

During the summer of 2021, I participated in the REU "Water Quality in the Yucatán Peninsula" program, led by Northern Illinois University.

The project focused on the Yucatán Peninsula, known for its vast karstic aquifer system supplying regional freshwater. I worked under Dr. Philip J. Carpenter, using specialized equipment like The SuperSting™ to conduct geophysical surveys near cenotes, sinkholes formed by collapsed limestone revealing groundwater.

These surveys helped us understand water flow, quality, and potential anthropogenic impacts due to tourism and migration. Notably, I discovered new locations, including Agua Azul, where I found water-filled conduits, fracture zones, and even an air cavity.

My findings were presented at the American Geophysical Union (AGU) and NDiSTEM - Society for Advancement of Chicanos/Hispanics & Native Americans in Science (SACNAS) conferences in 2021 and 2022, shedding light on the region's water dynamics and pollution concerns.

Teaching & Outreach

I hold a New York State teaching certification in Childhood Education (Grades 1–6) with a Bilingual Extension.

Over nine years as a second-grade bilingual educator, I developed culturally responsive literacy curricula that integrated urban lived experiences and cross-disciplinary content, using scaffolded instruction to support multilingual learners and students with diverse learning needs.

In addition to classroom teaching, I mentored one to two student-teachers annually, supporting their professional development through targeted guidance and pedagogical coaching. Although I am no longer actively teaching in the classroom, I remain deeply committed to fostering inclusive, supportive environments for students, mentees, and colleagues.
